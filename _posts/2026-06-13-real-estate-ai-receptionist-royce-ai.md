## The Hardest-Working Agent Still Misses Calls

In real estate, timing is everything. The National Association of Realtors has documented a statistic that every agent should know by heart: 78 percent of buyers ultimately work with the first agent who responds to their inquiry. Yet the median response time for a lead generated through Zillow, Realtor.com, or a brokerage website remains over four hours, and more than 40 percent of inbound leads never receive a response at all. This is not a reflection of agent laziness or incompetence. It is a structural problem inherent to the way real estate works.

Agents spend their days showing properties, attending closings, driving between listings, holding open houses, and meeting with sellers. During those hours, the phone rings. Maybe it is a buyer who just saw a new listing pop up on the MLS. Maybe it is a seller calling to ask about your commission structure. Maybe it is an inbound lead from the $1,500 monthly Zillow Premier Agent advertising campaign. Every call represents a potential commission, and every unanswered call represents a direct subtraction from the agent's bottom line.

The data from 2026 paints a sobering picture. According to research cited by the Everest Group and published by Goliath Data, real estate agents miss approximately 40 percent of inbound calls during standard business hours. When you extend the window to evenings and weekends — precisely when most serious buyers are searching — the numbers become far worse. An estimated 62 percent of seller inquiries arrive outside the traditional nine-to-five window, and 85 percent of callers who reach voicemail simply hang up and never call back. Each one of those missed calls represents an average of $7,500 in lost commission, and for a mid-volume agent or small team, the cumulative monthly toll can exceed $16,000.

## The Structural Problem With Human-Only Reception

The conventional solution to missed calls has been the human receptionist. Large brokerages employ dedicated front-desk staff, and top-producing agents sometimes hire personal assistants to screen and route calls. But the human-only model has fundamental limitations that no amount of training or overtime can overcome.

First, human receptionists cost money. A full-time receptionist in a mid-sized market commands a salary of $35,000 to $50,000 per year plus benefits, and that is for a single eight-hour shift. Covering evenings, weekends, and holidays requires either overtime pay, shift differentials, or multiple employees. For a small team or independent agent, that cost structure is simply unsustainable.

Second, human receptionists have natural cognitive limits. Even the most diligent receptionist can only handle one call at a time. During the peak hours of 10:00 AM to 2:00 PM on Saturdays — when open houses are running and new listings drop — a busy brokerage can receive three, four, or five calls simultaneously. The receptionist picks up one, and the others go to voicemail. Those callers, per the 85 percent rule, will not leave a message and will not call back. They will call the next agent on the search results page.

Third, human receptionists cannot intelligently qualify leads. A call comes in at 7:00 PM on a Tuesday. The receptionist has gone home. The call rolls to a voicemail box that fills up with unreturned messages. By the time the agent listens to them the next morning, the buyer has already scheduled a showing with a competitor who answered the phone.

Fourth, there is the issue of multilingual capacity. In any significant metropolitan market, a substantial portion of the buyer pool speaks Spanish, Mandarin, Korean, Vietnamese, or Tagalog as a first language. A human receptionist who speaks only English will struggle to engage those callers, and forcing them to leave a voicemail in a language they do not speak fluently is a guaranteed way to lose the lead permanently.
