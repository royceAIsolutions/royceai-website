# Veterinary Clinics Are Losing 843K Dollars to Missed Calls — Royce AI Captures Every Lead

## Introduction

Veterinary clinics operate on tight margins with overworked staff and overwhelmed phone lines. The average veterinary practice in the United States misses between 24 and 28 percent of all incoming calls, according to industry research from Puppilot and PeerLogic. That means for every four calls a clinic receives, at least one goes unanswered. In a busy practice seeing 1,500 active patients, that translates to hundreds of missed appointment opportunities every month. Pet owners calling to book a sick visit, refill a prescription, or ask about symptoms are met with busy signals and endless hold music.

The consequences are severe. A study by the American Veterinary Medical Association found that 81 percent of surveyed veterinarians report clients are more sensitive to costs than in 2024, making every missed call a lost revenue opportunity that clinics can no longer afford. When a concerned pet owner can not get through to schedule an appointment for their sick animal, they do not wait — they call the next clinic on their list.

## The Real Cost of Missed Pet Owner Calls

Research from AgentZap AI and PeerLogic has quantified the financial damage with alarming precision. Veterinary clinics lose an average of $843,000 annually due to missed calls when factoring in both immediate appointment revenue and long-term client lifetime value. For a single-location practice with an average transaction value of $212 per visit, missing just two calls per hour during business hours amounts to over $400,000 in direct lost revenue per year.

The problem intensifies after hours. Industry data from BCAT shows that 40 percent of veterinary calls arrive outside normal business hours. Most of these calls — whether from pet owners with a suddenly sick animal, a post-surgery complication concern, or a simple medication refill request — go straight to voicemail. The voicemail pick-up rate for veterinary clinics after hours is effectively zero. Pet owners who leave a voicemail after 5 PM and do not receive a callback until the next morning often have already taken their pet to an emergency hospital or a competing clinic.

## Why Traditional Phone Systems Fail Veterinary Practices

The standard veterinary clinic phone solution is a human receptionist sitting at the front desk, juggling walk-in traffic, processing payments, managing paperwork, and answering calls. During peak hours, especially Monday mornings when pet owners flood the practice with weekend-related health concerns, the phone system collapses under the load. The typical front desk team in a mid-sized veterinary clinic consists of two to three staff members. When both are occupied with in-person clients, the phone rings indefinitely or trips to voicemail.

Call forwarding services designed for veterinary practices offer a slight improvement but introduce a new set of problems. Most answering services do not have access to the clinic's scheduling system, so they can only take messages rather than book appointments. The pet owner leaves a message with a call center agent who has no medical knowledge and no context about the clinic's availability, leading to confusion and callback delays. Automated phone trees leave pet owners frustrated, with many hanging up before completing the menu.

In a 2025 survey from PetDesk, 69 percent of pet owners aged 18 to 34 and 63 percent of urban pet owners reported difficulty booking appointments with their veterinarian. This younger demographic is the fastest-growing segment of the pet ownership market, and they are increasingly choosing clinics based on convenience and responsiveness. A clinic that does not answer its phones is a clinic losing market share to competitors who do.
