# Veterinary Clinics Missed Calls — How Royce AI Saves Every New Pet Parent Lead

## The Cost of Missed Calls in Veterinary Medicine

Veterinary clinics operate on thin margins and high volume. A typical small animal practice sees 20 to 40 appointments per day, with each appointment representing $50 to $250 in revenue depending on the services provided. The front desk staff juggle check-ins, check-outs, prescription refills, surgical scheduling, and phone calls simultaneously. With this level of multitasking, it is no surprise that veterinary clinics miss an estimated 15 to 25 percent of incoming calls during business hours. After hours, the problem becomes even more severe — every single call goes unanswered unless the clinic has an emergency answering service.

The financial impact of missed calls is substantial. A veterinary clinic that generates $800,000 in annual revenue and misses 20 percent of incoming calls is losing approximately $160,000 in potential revenue each year. This is not theoretical — it is the direct result of calls that could have converted into new patient appointments, surgical consultations, and prescription refills going to voicemail or hitting a busy signal. For a clinic with a $50,000 monthly overhead, capturing just 10 percent of missed calls would add $15,000 to $20,000 per month to the bottom line.

## The Unique Challenge of Veterinary Client Communication

Veterinary medicine presents communication challenges that are distinct from human healthcare. Pet owners are often anxious and emotional when calling about a sick animal. They need reassurance, empathy, and clear instructions. A stressed pet parent who cannot reach a live person is likely to call the next clinic down the street. Research shows that 60 percent of veterinary clients who call a clinic and get voicemail will call a competitor before leaving a message.

The complexity of veterinary scheduling also increases the stakes. Unlike a restaurant reservation, a veterinary appointment involves triage — is this an emergency, a routine wellness visit, a surgical follow-up, or a dental cleaning? The receptionist must gather enough information to route the call appropriately. Automated phone trees and generic voicemail systems cannot handle this nuance, leading to frustrated callers and lost appointments.

## Why Traditional Solutions Fall Short

Many veterinary clinics rely on human receptionists during business hours and voicemail or answering services after hours. Each of these approaches has significant drawbacks:

Human receptionists are expensive and limited. A full-time veterinary receptionist costs $35,000 to $45,000 per year in salary plus benefits, and even the best receptionist can only answer one call at a time. During peak hours — typically 8 AM to 10 AM and 4 PM to 6 PM — call volume spikes, and clinics routinely hit the limit of how many calls a single person can handle.

Voicemail is the most common solution but the least effective. Studies show that 70 percent of pet owners who leave a voicemail for a veterinary clinic will also call another clinic. By the time the clinic returns the call, the pet has already been seen elsewhere. This is especially damaging for new client acquisition, as first-time callers have no loyalty to any particular clinic and will go wherever they can get through.

Third-party answering services charge $200 to $500 per month for basic coverage, but they employ generalists who have no veterinary medical knowledge. A caller asking about parvovirus symptoms or post-surgical vomiting cannot get meaningful triage from a general call center operator. The operator takes a message, and the clinic must call back anyway — adding latency to an already urgent situation.
