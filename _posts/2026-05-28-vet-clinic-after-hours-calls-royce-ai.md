# Vet Clinics Missing After-Hours Calls Lose Thousands in Revenue

After-hours pet emergencies do not wait for business hours. When a pet owner's dog eats chocolate at 9 PM on a Saturday or a cat sustains an injury at 6 AM on a Sunday, they pick up the phone immediately. If your veterinary clinic does not answer, they call the competitor who does. The harsh reality is that veterinary clinics across the country are losing thousands of dollars in monthly revenue simply because they cannot answer calls when the doors are closed.

The veterinary industry has a massive after-hours communication problem. Unlike human medical practices that often have on-call systems or emergency departments, most veterinary clinics operate on limited hours and rely on outdated voicemail systems or expensive third-party emergency services that patch calls to on-call veterinarians. Neither solution effectively captures the revenue and client loyalty that comes from handling after-hours inquiries properly.

## The After-Hours Veterinary Call Crisis

Veterinary clinics face a unique challenge in the healthcare industry. Pets get sick or injured at all hours, and pet owners appropriately view their animals as family members deserving immediate care. Research from the American Animal Hospital Association indicates that approximately 30 percent of pet health issues occur outside normal business hours. That translates to nearly one-third of potential patients and revenue walking away to competitors simply because no one answers the phone.

The financial impact is staggering when calculated over a typical month. Consider a mid-sized veterinary clinic averaging 3000 dollars per emergency visit. If the clinic misses just five after-hours calls per week because no one answers, that equals 20 missed opportunities per month. At 3000 dollars per case, that is 60000 dollars in lost monthly revenue. Over a year, the clinic loses 720000 dollars simply because they cannot answer the phone when pet owners need them most.

Beyond the immediate revenue loss, missed after-hours calls create long-term client attrition. Pet owners who cannot reach their regular veterinarian during an emergency often establish care with the emergency clinic they visited instead. Studies show that 40 percent of pet owners who switch to a new veterinary provider after an emergency visit never return to their original clinic. The lifetime value of a veterinary client averages 15000 dollars across the lifespan of their pet. Losing just ten clients per year to poor after-hours communication costs a clinic 150000 dollars in lifetime revenue.

## Why Traditional After-Hours Solutions Fail Veterinary Clinics

Veterinary clinics have attempted various solutions to address after-hours call handling, but each approach has significant drawbacks that ultimately fail to capture the revenue and client retention benefits of true 24/7 availability.

**Voicemail Systems:** The most common solution is also the worst. When pet owners call after hours and hear a voicemail greeting, 70 percent hang up without leaving a message. Those who do leave messages often wait hours for a return call, by which time they have already taken their pet elsewhere. Voicemail creates anxiety for pet owners who need immediate guidance on whether their pet's condition is life-threatening.

**Third-Party Emergency Services:** Some clinics contract with third-party answering services or emergency veterinary networks. These services typically charge 500 to 1000 dollars per month plus per-call fees. Worse, they often lack the specific knowledge about your clinic's services, policies, and staff that pet owners expect. Callers frequently complain that the person answering their call cannot answer basic questions about the clinic's location, hours, or services.

**On-Call Rotation Systems:** Many clinics rotate on-call duties among veterinarians and technicians. This approach creates burnout among staff who must be available 24/7, leading to high turnover. Additionally, on-call veterinarians cannot provide the immediate phone triage that pet owners want. They typically return calls within 30 to 60 minutes, by which time the pet owner has often already called another clinic.

**Forwarding to Cell Phones:** Some clinics forward after-hours calls to a rotating cell phone. This violates privacy regulations when client information is discussed on personal devices. It also creates an unprofessional impression when the answering veterinarian is in a noisy environment or cannot access the clinic's medical records system.
