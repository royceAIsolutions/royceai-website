# PART TWO: Stock Market Technical Analysis

This section is entirely dedicated to stock market technical analysis for research and learning purposes. It covers the fundamental concepts, indicators, chart patterns, and methodologies that traders and researchers use to analyze financial markets.

## What Is Technical Analysis?

Technical analysis is the study of price movements, volume, and chart patterns to predict future market direction. Practitioners believe that all known information is already reflected in the stock price, so analyzing the price action itself provides the best indication of what the market will do next.

This methodology applies to all liquid markets — stocks, ETFs, indices, forex, and cryptocurrencies. The foundation of technical analysis rests on three core principles:

1. **Market prices reflect all available information** — every known factor is already priced in
2. **Prices move in trends** — up, down, or sideways
3. **History repeats itself** — human psychology creates recognizable patterns

## Key Technical Indicators

### Relative Strength Index (RSI)

RSI measures the speed and magnitude of price movements on a scale of 0 to 100. Readings above 70 typically indicate overbought conditions, suggesting a price decline may be imminent. Readings below 30 suggest oversold conditions, indicating a potential bounce.

The standard RSI period is 14 days, but traders adjust this based on their timeframe. Day traders may use 7-period RSI for more sensitive signals, while swing traders might use 21-period RSI to filter out market noise.

### Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

MACD is a trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two moving averages of a security's price. The MACD line is the difference between the 12-day and 26-day exponential moving averages (EMAs). The signal line is a 9-day EMA of the MACD line.

MACD histograms visualize the distance between the MACD line and signal line. Expanding histograms indicate strengthening momentum, while contracting histograms suggest momentum is fading.

### Moving Averages

Moving averages smooth price data to identify trends:

- **20-day MA**: Short-term trend, closely watched by swing traders
- **50-day MA**: Medium-term trend, key support/resistance level
- **200-day MA**: Long-term trend, defines bull vs bear markets

When price trades above its moving averages, the trend is considered up. When below, the trend is down. Moving average crossovers generate buy or sell signals depending on the direction.

## Essential Chart Patterns

### Cup and Handle

The cup and handle is a bullish continuation pattern. The "cup" forms as price declines, bottoms out, and returns to the prior high. The "handle" is a brief consolidation that stays within the upper half of the cup. The breakout occurs when price moves above the handle's resistance on surging volume.

### Bull Flag

A bull flag appears after a sharp price advance. Price consolidates in a tight, slightly downward-sloping channel. The breakout occurs when price moves above the flag's upper trendline, resuming the uptrend. Volume should be low during formation and explode higher on breakout.

### Head and Shoulders

The head and shoulders pattern is a reliable trend reversal formation with three peaks: a higher middle peak (head) flanked by two lower peaks (shoulders). When price breaks below the neckline, it signals a trend reversal from bullish to bearish.

## Support and Resistance Levels

Support is a price level where buying interest overcomes selling pressure. Resistance is where selling pressure overcomes buying. These levels gain strength through multiple touches without breaking. Round numbers — such as 50, 100, 200 — often act as psychological support and resistance.

## Volume Analysis

Volume confirms price movements. Key principles include:

- **Volume precedes price** — Changes in volume often signal trend changes
- **Volume surges** — 50%+ above the 20-day average indicate institutional activity
- **Volume dry-ups** — Extremely low volume during pullbacks suggests weak selling pressure

Volume indicators like On-Balance Volume (OBV) track cumulative buying and selling pressure, often hinting at accumulation before breakouts.

## Candlestick Patterns

Japanese candlestick charts display open, high, low, and close prices. Key formations include:

- **Doji** — Open and close nearly equal, indicating indecision
- **Hammer** — Small body at top with long lower wick, bullish reversal signal
- **Shooting Star** — Small body at bottom with long upper wick, bearish reversal
- **Engulfing** — Large candle completely engulfs previous, strong reversal signal

## Timeframes in Technical Analysis

Different timeframes serve different purposes:

- **Weekly charts** — Identify primary trend, major support/resistance
- **Daily charts** — Time entries and exits, swing trading
- **Hourly charts** — Fine-tune entries, day trading
- **5-minute charts** — Scalping, precise timing

Multiple timeframe analysis improves accuracy when all timeframes align.

## Risk Management in Technical Analysis

Successful practitioners follow strict rules:

1. **Never risk more than 1-2% of your portfolio on a single trade**
2. **Use stop-loss orders** to define maximum loss
3. **Set profit targets** based on support/resistance
4. **Wait for confirmation** — never anticipate signals
5. **Cut losses quickly** — let winners run, kill losers fast

## Common Technical Analysis Mistakes

- **Overloading charts** — Too many indicators create conflicting signals
- **Ignoring the bigger trend** — Trading against the primary trend has lower probability
- **Chasing extended moves** — Buying after a 20% surge often means entering at the worst time
- **Setting stops too tight** — Normal market noise triggers stops prematurely
- **Forcing patterns** — Seeing formations that do not exist leads to false trades

## The Psychology of Technical Analysis

Every chart pattern reflects human psychology. Fear and greed drive market cycles. A cup and handle represents fear (decline), hope (recovery), hesitation (handle), and confidence (breakout).

Market psychology moves in cycles: accumulation (smart money buying), markup (public participation), distribution (smart money selling), and markdown (public panic). Recognizing which phase the market is in gives traders a significant edge.
