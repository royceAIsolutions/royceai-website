## Why Current Solutions Fail
Law firms have tried three main solutions to address language barriers, and all fall short:

### Human Receptionists
As mentioned, hiring multilingual staff is cost-prohibitive for most firms. Even if a firm hires a Spanish-speaking receptionist, they're still unable to serve Korean, Mandarin, or Vietnamese callers. Cross-training existing staff is rarely effective, as legal intake requires specialized vocabulary that part-time translators don't have.

### Third-Party Translation Services
Many firms use on-call translation services like LanguageLine, but these have major drawbacks. Calls take 2+ hours to schedule, which is too slow for urgent legal matters like personal injury or immigration cases. Translation services also cost 50 dollars per hour, adding up to 20,000 dollars per year for a mid-sized firm. Most importantly, 35% of callers hang up when told they need to wait for a translator, per a 2026 Inc. legal technology report.

### Voicemail Systems
Voicemail is the worst option of all. Non-English speakers are 4x more likely to hang up when they hear a voicemail prompt, according to the American Bar Association. For those who do leave messages, the quality of information is poor: 60% of voicemails from non-English speakers don't include a callback number, and 45% don't clearly state the legal issue.
