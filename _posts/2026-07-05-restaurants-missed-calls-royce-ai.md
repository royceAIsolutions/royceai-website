# Restaurants Lose $20 Billion Yearly to Missed Calls

## The $20 Billion Revenue Leak

Every day, across more than 700,000 restaurant locations in the United States, phones ring and no one answers. The customer on the other end wants to place a takeout order, book a reservation, or ask about catering. But the host is seating a party of six. The manager is handling a delivery dispute. The server is running food to table twelve. So the phone rings, and rings, and eventually the caller hangs up and calls the restaurant down the street.

This is not a small problem. According to a 2026 analysis by QSR Magazine based on data from the National Restaurant Association, Washington Hospitality Association, and TouchBistro, US restaurants collectively lose $20 billion in revenue every year to missed phone calls. This number is conservative. When factoring in that 81 percent of missed calls represent actual orders or reservations rather than the 60 percent industry baseline, the true figure approaches $24 billion annually.

For the average restaurant, the math is brutal. Industry data shows the typical location misses approximately 150 calls per month or 1,800 per year. At a 60 percent actionable rate, that is 1,080 lost opportunities annually. At a 70 percent conversion rate on those opportunities and an average order value of $38, the annual revenue loss per restaurant reaches $28,728. For pizza restaurants with high phone order volume, the number is significantly higher.

## When the Money Disappears

The timing of missed calls makes the problem worse. Peak call hours coincide exactly with peak service hours. Between 5 p.m. and 8 p.m., when restaurants are busiest serving in-person diners, 32 percent of all incoming calls go unanswered. Only one in three callers who get voicemail tries again. The rest redirect their business to a competitor.

This timing is critical because roughly 47 percent of a restaurant's daily phone orders come in during that exact three-hour window. The revenue is not being lost randomly throughout the day. It is vanishing during the hours that matter most to restaurant profitability.

The voicemail safety net that many operators rely on is largely an illusion. According to Bite Buddy's 2026 restaurant phone statistics report, 80 percent of callers who reach voicemail never leave a message. They simply hang up and move on. A voicemail system does not recover lost revenue. It only provides a timestamp for when the revenue walked out the door.

## Why Current Solutions Fail

Most restaurants have tried to solve the phone problem with traditional approaches, and none of them work well enough.

Hiring additional front-of-house staff dedicated to phone answering is expensive and unreliable. Labor costs already consume 30 to 35 percent of revenue in most full-service restaurants, and adding a dedicated phone operator at $15 to $20 per hour for peak shifts adds $1,200 to $1,600 per month in labor costs. Even then, that person cannot answer every call during a rush when they are also helping with walk-in guests.

Call centers and third-party answering services provide coverage but at significant cost. Professional restaurant call answering services charge $1,500 to $3,000 per month for basic coverage and often lack the specific knowledge needed to handle menu questions, modify orders, or manage reservation books accurately. Customers frequently complain about reading from scripts and inaccurate order taking.

Automated phone trees and IVR systems frustrate callers. The same Bite Buddy study found that 68 percent of restaurant callers prefer speaking to a human over navigating an automated menu. Long hold times during peak hours cause 45 percent of callers to hang up before reaching anyone at all.

Online ordering platforms help but do not solve the problem. Many customers still prefer calling, especially for catering orders, special requests, or when they have questions about menu items. Toast's 2025 Restaurant Technology Report confirmed that phone orders consistently produce higher average ticket sizes than online orders due to the conversational nature of the channel.
