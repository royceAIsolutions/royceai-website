## The Patient Portal Gap in 2026 Healthcare
Patient portals were originally designed to meet regulatory compliance requirements, not to improve clinical care or patient experience. A January 2026 CertifyHealth report notes that most portals remain disconnected from the day-to-day workflows of medical practices, leaving patients confused and staff overwhelmed. While 89% of US practices now offer portals per ONC data, only 25-30% of patients actively use them—leaving 70% of patients relying on phone calls for basic tasks like lab result checks, appointment scheduling, and prescription refills.

Industry data from GitNux’s 2026 patient engagement report shows that portal users achieve 72% higher satisfaction scores than non-users, but only when they can navigate the portal without friction. For the 70% of patients who don’t use portals regularly, satisfaction scores are 34% lower than average, driven by frustration with phone wait times and redundant paperwork.

For a mid-sized 12-provider multi-specialty practice, this disconnect translates to 1,800+ weekly calls about portal-related tasks, according to a March 2026 case study from US Tech Automations. Staff spend an average of 7 minutes per call assisting patients with portal login, form uploads, or navigation—adding up to 210 hours of lost productivity monthly that could be redirected to patient care.

## Why Patients Struggle With Standalone Portals
The core issue isn’t patient tech illiteracy, but a lack of integrated support. Most portals operate as standalone tools with no connection to a practice’s phone system or front-office workflow. When a patient calls with a portal question, staff must log into the portal separately, manually update records, and walk the patient through steps over the phone—a redundant process that wastes time for both parties.

Deloitte’s 2026 consumer engagement report highlights that 62% of patients find portal interfaces unintuitive, with 41% abandoning portal tasks midway due to confusion. For non-native English speakers, the barrier is even higher: PwC’s 2026 healthcare study found 47% of Spanish-speaking patients and 52% of Korean-speaking patients avoid portals entirely due to language gaps, leading to higher call volumes and lower engagement for diverse practices.

PwC’s study also notes that 58% of patients over age 65 find portal interfaces too small or hard to read, a gap Royce AI addresses by providing voice-guided navigation over the phone that doesn’t require patients to use the portal interface at all.
